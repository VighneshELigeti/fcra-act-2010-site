Banking Architecture Under FCRA

The 2020 amendments fundamentally restructured how FCRA money must flow. Today, every rupee of foreign contribution must enter India through one specific bank branch — SBI's New Delhi Main Branch — before moving anywhere else. This is the most operationally critical aspect of FCRA compliance.

⚠️ Why SBI New Delhi Main Branch Specifically?

The 2020 amendment designated SBI NDMB as the single choke point for all foreign contributions into India. This allows MHA and intelligence agencies to monitor all FCRA inflows in real time from one centralised location. Prior to 2020, organisations could designate any scheduled bank as their FCRA account — this decentralised model made monitoring difficult. The change was contentious as it increased operational inconvenience for organisations in remote areas.

Core Banking Rules — Don't Mix!

🚫
No Domestic Funds in FC Account
The FCRA receipt account and utilisation accounts must contain ONLY foreign contributions. Domestic funds (Indian donations, grants, service income) must never be deposited into FCRA accounts. Mixing = violation.
🚫
No FC in Domestic Account
Conversely, FC must never be deposited into the organisation's regular domestic bank accounts. If a foreign donor mistakenly sends money to a non-FCRA account, you must report it to MHA immediately and return or regularise the funds.
📊
Separate Books for FC
Accounting records for foreign contributions must be maintained separately. Even if your accounting software handles both, FC transactions must be 100% identifiable and traceable. FC accounts must be in prescribed format under FCRA Rules 2011.
🔒
No Sub-Granting (Post-2020)
Since the 2020 Amendment, an FCRA-registered organisation CANNOT transfer FC to any other organisation, even if the recipient is also FCRA-registered. This effectively killed the "sub-granting" or "pass-through" model. (Limited exceptions for relatives and specific cases.)
📈
No Speculative Investments
FC funds cannot be invested in stocks, shares, mutual funds, or any speculative activity. Idle FC funds can only earn interest in bank accounts. The interest earned on FC is itself treated as FC and must be used for the same purposes.
⚖️
Interest is Also FC
Interest earned on FC deposits is itself treated as foreign contribution and must be accounted for and used accordingly. It cannot be treated as general income and moved to domestic accounts. This applies from the moment interest is credited.

Scenario Explorer — Legal or Violation?

Click each scenario to understand whether it's permitted:

💸 NGO uses FC utilisation account to pay rent for its Delhi office LEGAL ✓ ▼
✓ Permitted. Programme-related expenses (office rent for FC-funded projects, staff salaries working on FC projects, project materials) can be paid from FCRA utilisation accounts. As long as the rent is genuinely for FCRA programme activities, this is fine. The 20% admin cap must, however, be monitored.
🏦 NGO asks US donor to wire transfer directly to its HDFC current account (non-FCRA) VIOLATION ✗ ▼
✗ Serious violation. Post-2020, all FC must arrive ONLY at the SBI NDMB designated FCRA receipt account. Receiving FC in any other account — even if the organisation has a valid FCRA registration — is a violation. The organisation must immediately report such receipt to MHA and return the funds. Criminal prosecution is possible.
🤝 FCRA-registered NGO "A" transfers ₹20 lakh to FCRA-registered NGO "B" for a joint project VIOLATION ✗ (Post-2020) ▼
✗ Violation since 2020 Amendment. Pre-2020, transfer between two FCRA-registered organisations was permitted with prior approval. The 2020 Amendment deleted this provision entirely. Today, no FCRA-registered entity can transfer FC to ANY other person/organisation. Both NGO A and B could face action. This change completely disrupted the Indian NGO ecosystem where larger NGOs would distribute grants to smaller field-level organisations.
💰 NGO deposits ₹5 lakh from Indian donors into FCRA utilisation account to cover urgent project costs VIOLATION ✗ ▼
✗ Mixing of funds — clear violation. Domestic funds (from Indian donors, grants, or income) can NEVER be deposited into FCRA accounts. FCRA accounts must contain only foreign contributions and interest earned on them. To cover both FC and domestic project costs, you need two parallel account systems. If the domestic project costs are also FC-purpose activities, use the correct FCRA account chain.
📈 NGO invests idle FC funds in a fixed deposit at a scheduled bank to earn higher returns GREY AREA / Partly OK ▼
⚠️ Permitted for FDs — speculative investments prohibited. FC funds can be placed in fixed deposits at scheduled banks to earn interest. This is not "speculative investment." However, investing FC in stocks, mutual funds, corporate bonds, or any market instrument is prohibited. The interest earned on FDs is itself treated as FC. The FD must be in the name of the FCRA-registered entity, and the interest must flow through the FCRA account chain.
👤 FCRA NGO transfers FC to a staff member's personal account to pay their salary LEGAL ✓ ▼
✓ Permitted — this is normal payroll. Salary payments to staff working on FCRA-funded projects from FCRA utilisation accounts are completely normal and legal. The restriction on "transfer" means donor-like re-grants to other organisations — not operational payments for services rendered. However, salaries must genuinely relate to FC-funded activities, and the 20% admin cap must be respected.

🚨 Biggest Trap: The Mixing Violation

The most common FCRA banking violation is inadvertent mixing — a domestic donor sends money to the FCRA utilisation account by mistake, or the organisation pays a common vendor from both domestic and FCRA accounts. Every instance of mixing — even accidental — is a legal violation. The remedy is to reverse the transaction immediately, maintain documentary evidence of the reversal, and report to MHA if the amount is significant. Prevention: maintain absolutely strict separation in your accounting and brief all staff on never depositing anything into FCRA accounts from domestic sources.

Utilisation Rules

Receiving FC is just the beginning. How you spend it is equally regulated. FCRA mandates that FC be used only for the purposes it was received, aligns spending with stated objectives, and caps administrative overhead at 20% — a rule with real teeth.

Admin expenses include: salaries of administrative staff, office rent, utilities, accounting, legal, travel for admin purposes, capital expenditure on office assets. Programme expenses: project staff salaries, beneficiary payments, field materials, programme travel — all directly tied to the stated FCRA purpose.

🚨 What Exactly Is "Administrative Expense"?

This is the most litigated question in FCRA. The FCRA Rules specify items that count as admin:

  • Salaries, allowances, payment to persons managing the organisation (management staff)
  • Rent, mortgage, utility costs for administrative premises
  • Purchase and maintenance of transport vehicles used for administration
  • Travel expenses for administrative purposes
  • Capital expenditure on objects of an administrative nature

Programme staff salaries are NOT admin expenses. The challenge: many salaries are split — the same person does both programme and admin work. Must be carefully documented and allocated.

FC can only be used for the purpose for which it was received. FCRA §11(2) specifies the allowed categories:

⚠️ The "Purpose Deviation" Trap

FC received for "education" CANNOT be spent on healthcare — even if both are noble goals. If a donor gives money for a school building and the organisation uses some of it for a medical camp, that's a purpose deviation. The fix: either return the excess to the donor, get the donor's written consent to change purpose, or maintain absolutely strict project-wise accounting. MHA can demand refund of diverted FC.

Permitted vs Prohibited — Spending Categories

✅ Permitted Uses

✓Programme staff salaries and benefits
✓Purchase of project materials, equipment
✓Construction / renovation of programme facilities (schools, clinics)
✓Travel for field work / programme activities
✓Subcontracting to service providers (vendors)
✓Bank fixed deposits (to earn interest on idle FC)
✓Admin expenses up to 20% of FC received
✓Refund to donor with prior intimation to MHA

🚫 Prohibited Uses

✗Transfer to another organisation (post-2020)
✗Investment in stocks, mutual funds, speculative assets
✗Use for purposes different from stated FCRA purpose
✗Admin expenses exceeding 20% of FC received
✗Mixing with domestic funds in same accounts
✗Paying bribes, illegal gratification
✗Creating unused reserves without planned purpose
✗Converting FC to bearer instruments/cash for others

Frequently Confused Spending Categories

💻 Computers / Laptops — Programme or Admin? ▼
It depends on who uses them.

A laptop used exclusively by a field social worker for recording beneficiary data = programme expense.
A laptop used by the CEO / finance manager = admin expense.
A laptop shared between both = must be allocated proportionally (e.g., 60% admin, 40% programme based on usage logs).

Maintain an asset register with usage classification. Auditors will look at this closely.
🚗 Vehicle expenses — Programme or Admin? ▼
Field programme vehicle (e.g., ambulance, community outreach van): Programme expense.
Office/admin vehicle used by management for city travel: Admin expense.
Multi-purpose vehicle: Must be log-booked and split by usage — programme km vs admin km.

Best practice: maintain a vehicle log book showing date, driver, destination, purpose (programme/admin), and kilometres.
🏠 Rent for NGO office — Programme or Admin? ▼
Admin office rent (for management, accounting, HR): Admin expense — counts toward the 20% cap.
Rent for field office / programme centre: Programme expense if it's exclusively for programme implementation.
Combined use office: Must be allocated based on area or usage ratio.

Many small NGOs try to classify all rent as "programme expense" to avoid hitting the admin cap — this is risky. MHA auditors are trained to look for this.
👤 Director / CEO salary — Programme or Admin? ▼
The salary of the Executive Director / CEO / Managing Trustee is generally classified as an administrative expense since their primary role is organisational leadership and management.

However, if the ED also directly implements programmes (e.g., a doctor who is also the MD of a health NGO and sees patients), that portion can be split.

Safe approach: Classify the full ED salary as admin unless supported by time-sheet records showing clear programme delivery time.
📊 Audit / accounting fees — Programme or Admin? ▼
Statutory audit fees, FCRA compliance costs, annual return filing: Admin expense — counts toward the 20% cap.

Impact evaluation / programme assessment costs: Could be classified as programme expense if it's evaluating programme outcomes — keep supporting documentation.

FCRA compliance costs themselves are often 5-10% of the admin budget. For small organisations receiving modest FC, the 20% cap can be genuinely constraining when audit + compliance + management is factored in.

The Sub-Granting Collapse (2020)

This is one of the most impactful changes in modern FCRA and affects almost every major NGO:

PRE-2020: Sub-Granting Permitted
Large "apex" FCRA-registered NGOs could receive FC and distribute it to smaller field-level NGOs with prior MHA approval.

Model: International NGO → Large Indian NGO → 50 small village-level NGOs

This enabled scale, reach, and grassroots implementation through a network model.
POST-2020: Transfer Completely Prohibited
Section 7 was amended to prohibit transfer of FC to ANY person/organisation — even another FCRA-registered entity.

Impact: Large NGOs that operated as "fund distributors" had to either become direct implementers or stop operations. Many village-level NGOs lost access to foreign funding entirely because they lacked the capacity to independently apply for FCRA.

Result: Severe disruption of the Indian civil society ecosystem.
⚠️ The 2022 SC Clarification on Sub-Granting

In Noel Harper vs. Union of India (2022), the Supreme Court upheld the 2020 Amendment's ban on sub-granting as constitutional, noting that the government has the right to regulate how foreign money flows through civil society. Several civil society petitions challenged this. The court held the national security rationale sufficient. The ban stands.

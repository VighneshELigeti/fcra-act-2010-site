Overview & Objectives of FCRA 2010

FCRA is India's gatekeeper for foreign money. Before any foreign funds can enter Indian civil society, they must pass through a legal checkpoint. This module explains why that checkpoint exists and what it's trying to protect.

Why does FCRA exist?

Imagine a foreign government wanting to influence an Indian election by quietly funding a political party. Or a foreign foundation pumping money into media organisations to shape narratives. Or foreign intelligence services bankrolling groups that destabilise border states. FCRA was enacted to prevent exactly these scenarios.

💡 Simple Analogy

Think of FCRA as a customs checkpoint for money. Just like you can't bring certain items into India without declaring them or getting permission, organisations can't bring foreign funds into India without first obtaining FCRA clearance. The checkpoint exists to protect India's internal affairs from external influence.

The Six Pillars FCRA Protects

The statement of objects and reasons in FCRA 2010 identifies six key areas the law aims to safeguard. Click each pillar to learn more:

🇮🇳
Sovereignty & Integrity
No foreign destabilisation
Foreign funds must not be used to challenge India's territorial integrity, undermine the constitution, or foment secessionism in any region.
🛡️
Security & Defence
Strategic interests protected
Funds cannot support activities that harm India's defence establishment, military interests, or strategic partnerships.
⚖️
Public Interest
General welfare first
Foreign money must serve legitimate public welfare objectives — education, health, environment, culture — not covert or harmful purposes.
🗳️
Electoral Politics
Clean elections mandate
Political parties, election candidates, and closely allied organisations are prohibited from receiving foreign funds to keep elections free from foreign influence.
🕌
Communal Harmony
Unity protection
Foreign funds cannot be used to stoke religious or communal tensions, fund hate groups, or undermine India's pluralistic social fabric.
🏗️
Sensitive Domains
Media, judiciary, etc.
Certain sensitive domains like journalism, the judiciary, and the civil services are ring-fenced from foreign funding to preserve institutional independence.

A Brief History: From 1976 to 2010

1976

FCRA 1976 enacted

The original FCRA was passed during India's Emergency period amid concerns about foreign funding influencing domestic politics. It laid the foundation for the current framework.

1984–2000s

Growing civil society + liberalisation

India's opening economy and booming NGO sector meant more foreign funds flowing in. The 1976 Act began showing its age — it lacked provisions for online filing, account-specific banking, etc.

2010

FCRA 2010 replaces FCRA 1976

A comprehensive overhaul. FCRA 2010 introduced designated banking architecture, stricter eligibility norms, electronic filing, and clearer enforcement provisions. The 1976 Act was repealed.

2016

First major amendment

Retrospectively changed the definition of "foreign source" to bring SBI and other nationalised banks into conformity. Also addressed political party funding concerns after a Delhi HC ruling.

2020

FCRA Amendment Act 2020 — major tightening

Introduced: mandatory SBI New Delhi Main Branch as the single designated receipt account, Aadhaar-based KYC for office bearers, ban on sub-granting to other organisations, and reduced admin cap from 50% to 20%.

What does FCRA actually regulate?

📥 Acceptance of Foreign Contribution ▼
FCRA regulates the act of receiving foreign contribution into India. It mandates that any eligible entity must only receive FC via the prescribed SBI FCRA account. Even if all other conditions are met, bypassing the designated bank route is a violation. The focus is on controlling the entry point.
💰 Utilisation of Foreign Contribution ▼
FCRA regulates not just receipt, but also how the money is spent. It must be used only for the purposes stated in the registration/prior permission application. Administrative expenses are capped at 20%. Spending on speculative investments or prohibited activities is banned.
🔄 Transfer of Foreign Contribution ▼
Post-2020, FCRA registered organisations can no longer transfer FC funds to other persons or organisations (whether FCRA registered or not). This was one of the most significant changes — it ended the practice of "sub-granting" where large NGOs distributed FC to smaller grassroots organisations.
📋 Reporting and Accountability ▼
Every FCRA-registered entity must file an annual return (FC-4) online by 31st December, disclosing all receipts and utilisation for the preceding financial year. Changes in office bearers, bank accounts, and registered addresses must be reported via intimations on the FCRA portal.

FCRA vs Other Laws

FCRA sits alongside other laws that govern foreign investments and dealings:

Law What it covers Regulator Applies to NGOs?
FCRA 2010 Foreign contributions (donations/grants) to civil society MHA YES (primary)
FEMA 1999 Foreign exchange transactions — trade, investment, capital RBI / ED Partially
PMLA 2002 Money laundering — tracking suspicious funds ED / FIU If violations involve laundering
Income Tax Act Taxation of all receipts including FC (unless exempt) CBDT Yes (sec 11/12 exemptions)
⚠️ Common Confusion

FCRA and FEMA are often confused. FEMA covers commercial transactions and capital flows. FCRA covers donations and grants. A foreign company paying you for services (at arm's length) is generally governed by FEMA, not FCRA. But the boundary can be tricky — always check the specific transaction against FC definitions.

The Three Questions FCRA Forces You to Ask

❓
Is this "Foreign Contribution"?
Donation? Gift? Grant? Or commercial?
The first question is always: does the payment/receipt constitute "foreign contribution" under the FCRA definition? If yes, FCRA applies. If it's an arm's-length commercial payment, FEMA applies instead.
❓
Is the recipient eligible?
Prohibited person? Or eligible entity?
Even if the payment is FC, some recipients are absolutely prohibited. Others need registration or prior permission. Eligibility is the second key question.
❓
Are all procedures followed?
Banking, reporting, utilisation?
Even eligible entities can violate FCRA by using wrong bank accounts, exceeding admin caps, missing annual returns, or sub-granting funds.

"FCRA is essentially India's sovereignty firewall for the non-profit sector. It doesn't ban foreign philanthropy — it channels and monitors it."

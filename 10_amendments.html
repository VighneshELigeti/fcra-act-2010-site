Key Amendments to FCRA 2010

FCRA has been significantly amended twice since 2010 — once in 2016 (focused on political party funding) and again in 2020 (a sweeping restriction of NGO operations). Each amendment introduced changes that fundamentally restructured how foreign money flows into Indian civil society.

FCRA Legislative Timeline

1976
FCRA 1976 — Original Act
Enacted during the Emergency period under Indira Gandhi. First comprehensive law to regulate foreign contributions. Focused on preventing foreign interference in Indian politics and elections.
2010
FCRA 2010 — Complete Recodification
The 1976 Act was repealed and replaced with the Foreign Contribution (Regulation) Act, 2010. Key additions: mandatory SBI account (then any scheduled bank), 5-year validity, online portal, clearer definitions, enhanced enforcement powers, ban on political party FC.
2016
FCRA Amendment Act 2016
Focused on political party funding. Removed cap on foreign electoral bonds. Re-defined "foreign company" to retrospectively exempt banks with government equity. Allowed electoral bonds as vehicle for political party funding.
2020
FCRA Amendment Act 2020 — Most Significant
Sweeping changes affecting all FCRA-registered NGOs. Mandatory SBI NDMB account. Ban on sub-granting. Admin cap reduced to 20%. Aadhaar KYC mandatory. Definition of "foreign source" expanded. Supreme Court upheld all changes in Noel Harper (2022).
2022
Noel Harper vs. Union of India — SC Verdict
Supreme Court upheld all 2020 Amendment provisions as constitutional. Held that receiving foreign contributions is not a fundamental right. Dismissed petitions from multiple civil society organisations challenging the amendments.

The 2016 Amendment was smaller in scope — primarily addressing political party funding and retrospectively regularising banking-related issues:

Major Redefinition of "foreign source" — Banks Exempted Retrospectively §2(1)(j) ▼
PRE-2016
A "foreign company" (which includes Indian companies with 50%+ foreign equity) was a "foreign source." Banks like SBI where the government holds >50% stake are technically still Indian. But private banks with significant FII investment could be seen as foreign-owned. Donations to political parties through such banks were questioned.
POST-2016
Amendment added a proviso excluding any company where the majority holding vests with the Indian government. More critically, the definition was amended retrospectively to exempt banks (specifically in the context of political party donations via electoral bonds) from the "foreign source" classification, preventing challenges to past political party donations.
Controversy: The 2016 amendment was challenged before the Delhi HC and later the SC by the Association for Democratic Reforms and Bharatiya Janata Party / Congress controversy — both major parties had received funds from a company with >50% foreign shareholding (Vedanta) before the 2016 amendment. The retrospective change was widely seen as self-serving by the ruling party.
Major Electoral Bonds — New Vehicle for Political FC Political Finance ▼
Electoral bonds were introduced in 2018 as part of Budget amendments (not directly FCRA, but FCRA 2016 laid groundwork). The FCRA 2016 amendment removed the requirement that political parties disclose donations above ₹20,000 when received via electoral bonds.
2024 Update: The Supreme Court struck down electoral bonds in February 2024 (Association for Democratic Reforms case, 5-judge constitutional bench) as unconstitutional — violating the right to information of voters. SBI was ordered to disclose all electoral bond data. The FCRA-political party nexus via electoral bonds is now judicially invalidated.
Admin Surrender of FCRA Registration — New Provision §14A (new) ▼
2016 Amendment introduced §14A allowing an organisation to voluntarily surrender its FCRA registration. This creates an orderly exit mechanism — organisations that no longer need FC access or want to avoid compliance burden can surrender rather than simply stop filing.

The 2020 Amendment was the most significant overhaul since 2010. It made FCRA compliance dramatically more restrictive and operationally burdensome:

Major Mandatory SBI New Delhi Main Branch Account §17 — New Proviso ▼
BEFORE 2020
FC could be received in a designated account at ANY scheduled commercial bank chosen by the organisation. An NGO in Chennai could use a Chennai-based bank branch.
AFTER 2020
ALL FC must first come to State Bank of India, New Delhi Main Branch (Branch Code: 00691) — one single account for every FC. FC can then be transferred to utilisation accounts at any bank. This is mandatory regardless of where the NGO operates.
Operational impact: Small NGOs in remote areas had to travel to Delhi / establish SBI ties. Many NGOs had to restructure entire banking relationships. The government's stated rationale: centralised monitoring of all FC inflows.
Major Complete Ban on FC Transfer Between Organisations §7 — Amended ▼
BEFORE 2020
An FCRA-registered organisation could transfer up to 50% of its FC to another FCRA-registered organisation with prior MHA approval. This enabled apex NGOs to distribute grants to smaller implementing partners.
AFTER 2020
ABSOLUTE PROHIBITION on transfer to any person or any association — whether registered or not. The "50% with approval" route is completely gone. No exceptions (except individuals for personal use, not for further FC purposes).
Impact: Organisations like Oxfam India, Aga Khan Foundation, CARE India — which operated as "intermediary" grant-makers — had to completely restructure. Hundreds of small grassroots NGOs that depended on sub-grants lost access to foreign funding. The Supreme Court in Noel Harper (2022) upheld this as a valid restriction.
Major Admin Expense Cap Reduced from 50% to 20% §8(1)(b) — Amended ▼
BEFORE 2020
Maximum 50% of FC could be used for administrative expenses. This was considered relatively generous, allowing NGOs to maintain adequate overhead for quality programme delivery.
AFTER 2020
Maximum 20% — a 60% reduction (from 50% to 20%). For organisations with high overhead due to complex operations, this has forced difficult choices: reduce admin costs, reclassify expenses as programmatic, or face violations.
Major Aadhaar KYC Mandatory for All Office Bearers §12(1A), §12A — New ▼
New §12A requires every office bearer, key functionary, and member to provide Aadhaar as identity proof. For foreign nationals: passport. This applies to all applications (new registration, prior permission, renewal). Any existing registration must update Aadhaar details for all office bearers.
Impact: Many legacy NGO trustees (elderly individuals, overseas trustees without Aadhaar) created compliance problems. NGOs had to remove or bring on board new trustees to comply. Applications with even one missing Aadhaar are rejected.
Moderate Expansion of "Foreign Source" Definition §2(1)(j) — Amended ▼
The definition of "foreign source" was expanded to include companies where even a minority stake is foreign — effectively lowering the threshold for triggering FCRA. This affects many Indian companies that have any degree of foreign institutional investment.
Moderate SC Verdict: All 2020 Changes Upheld Noel Harper vs. UoI (2022) ▼
In Noel Harper vs. Union of India, a 5-judge constitutional bench of the Supreme Court (April 2022) upheld all provisions of the FCRA Amendment Act 2020 as constitutionally valid. Key holdings:

• Receiving foreign contribution is NOT a fundamental right
• FCRA is a regulatory measure, not a restriction on fundamental rights
• The state has the right to control foreign money flows in civil society
• Ban on sub-granting is a reasonable restriction
• SBI mandate is operational, not unconstitutional

The verdict effectively closed the door on constitutional challenges to FCRA's stringent provisions.
🚨 Exam Focus: What Changed in 2020

The 5 most-tested 2020 changes:

  1. SBI New Delhi Main Branch — mandatory FCRA receipt account
  2. Sub-granting / transfer to other NGOs — completely prohibited
  3. Admin cap — reduced from 50% to 20%
  4. Aadhaar KYC — mandatory for all office bearers
  5. Noel Harper (2022) — SC upheld all changes as constitutional

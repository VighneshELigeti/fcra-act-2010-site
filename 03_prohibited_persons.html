Module 03

Prohibited Persons — The Hard NO List

Section 3 of FCRA creates an absolute prohibition — certain categories of persons can NEVER receive foreign contributions, regardless of how legitimate the purpose might seem. There is no registration, no prior permission, no exemption for them.

Section 3 — The Complete Prohibited List

Click each category to understand who exactly is covered and why:

BANNED
🗳️
Election Candidates
§3(1)(a)
Who: Any person who is a candidate for election to any legislature at the time of receiving FC.

Why prohibited: Direct funding of election candidates from foreign sources would allow foreign governments and entities to directly influence who wins Indian elections — the most fundamental attack on democratic sovereignty.

Note: Applies while the person IS a candidate. Post-election status may change the analysis, but political sensitivities remain.
BANNED
🏛️
Political Parties & Members
§3(1)(b)
Who: Political parties and their office bearers. Any person holding office in a political party.

Why prohibited: No foreign funding of political parties — period. This is a cornerstone of FCRA's democratic protection mandate. Foreign-funded political parties would be susceptible to foreign policy manipulation.

The 2016 Amendment angle: Banks with partial government ownership were retrospectively removed from the "foreign company" definition so that historic donations to political parties via banking channels could be regularised. This remains controversial.
BANNED
⚖️
Judges & Magistrates
§3(1)(c)
Who: Judges and any person employed in any judicial capacity, including magistrates.

Why prohibited: Judicial independence is non-negotiable. Foreign funding of judges — even for "research" or "conferences" — would create conflicts of interest and potentially compromise judicial impartiality in cases involving foreign parties.

Practical implication: Even attending a foreign-funded judicial conference where individual judges receive payments falls under scrutiny.
BANNED
👨‍💼
Government Servants
§3(1)(d)
Who: Any person employed by or in relation to any undertaking of the government — from IAS officers to Class IV employees of central/state governments, PSUs, local bodies.

Why prohibited: Government employees making policy decisions, giving licences, or implementing programmes must not be financially beholden to foreign interests. Foreign funding creates a clear conflict of interest.

Scope question: PSU employees and employees of government-funded organisations may also fall within this ambit — verify on specific facts.
BANNED
📺
Media (As Specified)
§3(1)(e)/(f)
Who: Persons specified in the Act — historically includes certain categories of media professionals (editors, owners, publishers of print/broadcast media as notified or as covered by the broad provisions).

Why prohibited: Foreign-funded media would be tools of foreign propaganda, capable of shaping public opinion in favour of foreign interests. A free press must be financially independent of foreign governments.

Note: Media organisations (companies) must separately ensure their ownership doesn't make them a "foreign source" — the 50% foreign shareholding rule is critical here.
BANNED
🏢
Political Nature Orgs
§3(1)(f) + §5
Who: Organisations of a "political nature" as notified by the Central Government under §5. This is a discretionary power — any civil society organisation can be notified as "political nature" based on its actual activities.

Why prohibited: To prevent circular routing — foreign money can't go directly to a political party, but also can't go to a front/allied organisation that will then funnel it for political purposes.

⚠️ Very Important: Greenpeace India was previously listed as "political nature." Several activists and advocacy organisations have been so notified. The test is the actual activities, not just the stated objectives.

The Critical Distinction: Prohibited vs Restricted

🚫 §3 — Absolutely Prohibited

  • No registration possible
  • No prior permission available
  • No exemption, even for "good causes"
  • Even receiving the FC accidentally is a violation
  • The donor may also be liable
  • Criminal prosecution possible
Example: An election candidate cannot get an FCRA registration because they are simply prohibited, not merely ineligible for registration.

⚠️ §4+§10 — Eligible but Needs Permission

  • CAN receive FC with proper authorisation
  • Registration route: ongoing permission
  • Prior permission route: one-time project
  • Must maintain compliance continuously
  • Subject to inspection and audit
  • Registration can be cancelled for violations
Example: A new NGO without a 3-year track record can apply for prior permission for a specific project, even if they can't get full registration yet.
🚨 The "Political Nature" Trap — Most Dangerous Pitfall

An organisation doing legitimate social work can be suddenly notified as "political nature" by the government, retroactively making any received FC illegal from the date of notification. There is no advance warning.

Signs that increase "political nature" risk: organising protests, opposing government policies publicly, filing PILs against the government, issuing manifestos on governance, influencing elections even indirectly. The "political nature" label is applied based on actual activities, not just stated objectives.

Why These Categories? — The Underlying Logic

Quick Knowledge Check

🧠 Test Your Understanding

Q1: An MLA (Member of the Legislative Assembly) wants to receive a grant from a US foundation for his constituency's welfare work. Can he?
A. Yes, if he applies for prior permission from MHA
B. No — members of legislature are prohibited under §3
C. Yes, if it's for a charitable purpose like education
D. Only if approved by the State Government
✓ Correct! Members of legislature — MPs, MLAs, MLCs — are absolutely prohibited under Section 3. There is no route (registration, prior permission, government approval) that makes it legal. The purpose (constituency welfare, charity) is irrelevant. The prohibition is absolute.
Q2: A retired High Court judge (now in private legal practice) receives a speaking honorarium from a foreign law firm. Is this a concern under FCRA?
A. Yes, judges are permanently banned from receiving anything foreign
B. Depends — if "retired" they may no longer be "employed in judicial capacity", but the nature of the payment matters
C. No concern — honorariums are excluded from FCRA
D. Only concern if the payment exceeds ₹10 lakh
✓ Correct! The §3 prohibition applies to those "employed in any judicial capacity." A retired judge in private practice may no longer be in judicial capacity. BUT: if the honorarium is for attending a conference (donation-like) vs. legal advice (commercial service), the FC analysis changes. The key question is: is this a "donation/delivery" (FC) or a commercial payment (FEMA)?
Q3: A prominent environmental NGO organised protests against a government infrastructure project. The government is considering notifying it as "political nature." If notified, what happens to its existing FCRA registration?
A. Nothing — existing registration remains valid
B. Registration continues with restrictions on new donations
C. Registration becomes void/liable for cancellation — further receipt of FC becomes illegal
D. Organisation gets 2 years to convert to political party
✓ Correct! Once an organisation is notified as "political nature," it becomes prohibited under §3 and its FCRA registration becomes liable for cancellation. Any subsequent receipt of FC after notification is illegal. The MHA will typically suspend/cancel the registration following such a notification.
📌 Notable Case: Greenpeace India

Greenpeace India was placed on a watchlist and its FCRA registration was suspended/cancelled following intelligence inputs that it had engaged in activities affecting national interest (specifically opposing nuclear power plants and coal mining). The case highlighted the breadth of the "public interest" and "political nature" grounds, and how advocacy organisations can be vulnerable to FCRA action even when conducting what they consider purely environmental work. The Greenpeace case is a landmark example of the intersection of FCRA enforcement and civil society freedoms.

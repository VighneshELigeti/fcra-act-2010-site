Key Definitions in FCRA

In law, definitions are everything. FCRA's scope — who it applies to, what it covers, who can receive what — all flows from four foundational definitions. Get these right and everything else becomes logical.

The four definitions that control FCRA's universe are:

Foreign Contribution Foreign Source Person Association
💰
1. "Foreign Contribution" (FC)
Section 2(1)(h) — The most important definition
▼
§ 2(1)(h) — Statutory Definition (simplified)
"Foreign contribution" means the donation, delivery or transfer made by any foreign source of— (i) any article, not being an article given to a person as a gift for personal use, whose market value at the time of importation exceeds [prescribed limit]; (ii) any currency, whether Indian or foreign; (iii) any security as defined in the Securities Contracts (Regulation) Act.

Breaking it down — the THREE Forms of FC

💵 Currency (most common)

Any donation of money — Indian rupees or foreign currency — from a foreign source. This is the most common form of FC. Bank transfers, cheques, drafts all count.

📦 Articles (goods)

Donated goods from foreign sources — computers, medical equipment, vehicles. BUT: personal gifts below the market value threshold are excluded.

📈 Securities

Shares, bonds, debentures donated by a foreign source. The market value transfer is treated as FC. Rare in practice but legally relevant.

❌ What is NOT FC

Commercial payments for services at arm's length, salary from a foreign employer for legitimate work, payments under international contracts — generally NOT FC (FEMA governs instead).

The "FC or Not FC?" Test — Interactive Examples

✓ IS Foreign Contribution
  • US foundation donates $50,000 to an Indian NGO for education
  • German church grants €30,000 to an Indian trust for tribal welfare
  • NRI sends ₹5 lakh "donation" to his village's charitable society
  • UK NGO donates 10 laptops to Indian school (above prescribed value)
  • Foreign company gives corporate social responsibility grant
✗ NOT Foreign Contribution (FEMA, not FCRA)
  • Foreign company pays Indian IT firm for software services
  • Indian subsidiary receiving FDI from parent company
  • Payment to Indian lawyer for legal services by foreign client
  • Royalties from foreign publisher to Indian author for book
  • NRI sends money to family for household expenses
⚡ Tricky Boundary Case: Scholarship / Fellowship

A foreign university giving a scholarship directly to an individual Indian student — not FC (it's for personal educational purpose). BUT if it's channelled through an Indian NGO that "selects" and distributes scholars — it could be FC. The nature of the transaction (who receives, for what purpose) matters enormously.

⚡ The NRI Trap

A Non-Resident Indian (NRI) sending money to an Indian association is treated as a foreign source. So if your NGO's trustee who is an NRI donates ₹2 lakh to the trust — that is FC. Many small organisations get caught by this. A person of Indian origin holding foreign citizenship is a foreign source.

2. "Foreign Source"
Section 2(1)(j) — Who counts as a foreign source?
▼
§ 2(1)(j) — Foreign Source includes:
(i) the Government of any foreign country or territory and any agency of such Government;
(ii) any international agency, not being the United Nations or any of its specialised bodies / the World Bank / IMF;
(iii) a foreign company;
(iv) a corporation company incorporated in a foreign country;
(v) a multi-national corporation;
(vi) a company within the meaning of the Companies Act 2013, where more than one-half of its nominal value of share capital is held by foreign sources;
(vii) a foreign trust or foundation;
(viii) a foreign citizen (including PIO/OCI);

3. "Person"
Section 2(1)(p) — Broader than you think
▼
§ 2(1)(p)
"Person" includes—(i) an individual; (ii) a Hindu undivided family; (iii) an association; (iv) a company registered under section 8 of the Companies Act 2013.

Why does this matter?

The broad definition of "person" means FCRA doesn't just apply to NGOs. An individual Indian citizen can also be caught by FCRA if foreign funds are routed to them in a donation capacity.

⚠️ Common Mistake

People think FCRA only applies to "NGOs." Not true. Any person receving foreign contribution is regulated. A Hindu Undivided Family receiving a donation from a non-resident relative, an academic individual receiving foreign research grants —these can all fall under FCRA's ambit.

4. "Association" & "Political Nature"
Section 2(1)(b) & 2(1)(f) — Who needs registration?
▼
§ 2(1)(b) — "Association"
"Association" means an association of individuals, whether incorporated or not, having an office in India and carrying on activities in India. Includes: trusts, societies, Section 8 companies, registered unregistered bodies.

The "Political Nature" Trap — Section 2(1)(f)

§ 2(1)(f)
"Organisation of a political nature" means any organisation, other than a political party, which the Central Government may notify from time to time as an organisation of a political nature, having regard to its activities, objectives, the method of operation, and other relevant factors.

The government has wide discretion to notify any organisation as "political nature" — which then disqualifies it from receiving foreign contributions. This is one of the most controversial aspects of FCRA and has been used against several civil society organisations.

🚨 Critical Pitfall

An organisation doing advocacy, lobbying, or "political" activities (even if not a formal political party) can be notified as "political nature" and lose its FCRA eligibility. The boundary between "civic engagement" and "political activity" is not always clear, making this one of FCRA's most risky grey zones.

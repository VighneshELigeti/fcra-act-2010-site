Module 09

Enforcement & Penalties

FCRA is a criminal statute — violations can result in seizure of assets, heavy fines, and imprisonment up to 5 years. The Ministry of Home Affairs has wide investigative and enforcement powers, and individuals (not just organisations) face personal liability.

Enforcement Machinery

🔍
§22 — Inspection
MHA-Authorised Inspector Powers
MHA can authorise an "authorised officer" to inspect books of accounts, documents, assets and property of any registered or formerly registered entity. Officers have broad access rights — inspection can be unannounced. You cannot obstruct an inspection (that itself is an offence).
📂
§23 — Search & Seizure
Search of Premises & Seizure of Documents
Authorised officers can enter, search, and seize any document, book of accounts, or property where there is reasonable belief that evidence of violation exists. A panchnama must be prepared. Seized material can be retained for investigation. No court warrant is required for this.
💰
§25 — Freezing of Bank Accounts
Account Freeze During Investigation
MHA can direct any bank to freeze FCRA accounts pending investigation. No notice to the organisation is required before freezing. Organisation must then apply to MHA for permission to withdraw even for essential operating expenses. This can be devastating for day-to-day operations.
⚖️
§26 — Confiscation
FC or Assets May Be Confiscated
Any FC received in violation, or any property acquired from such FC, can be confiscated. The confiscation order is made by an "adjudicating authority" — not a court. This can include cash balances, bank deposits, vehicles, real estate.
🚨
§28 — Vesting in Government
Assets Vest in Central Government
Post-cancellation, any FC and property acquired therefrom may be ordered to vest in the Central Government. This is separate from confiscation and is the ultimate financial consequence of cancellation.
🏛️
§34 — Prosecution & Courts
Criminal Trial Before Sessions Court
FCRA offences are triable by a Sessions Court. The complaint must be filed by, or on the written authorisation of, MHA (or an officer not below Joint Secretary). The offences are non-bailable where imprisonment exceeds 3 years.

Offences & Penalties — Chapter VII

§35
General FCRA Violations
Fine + Imprisonment up to 5 years + Forfeiture of FC
The catch-all provision. Covers: accepting FC without registration/PP, using FC for wrong purpose, violating any FCRA provision.
§35(3)
Operating After Cancellation
Fine + Imprisonment up to 5 years
Continuing to receive or use FC after registration is cancelled. One of the most serious offences — often treated as wilful defiance.
§36
Obstructing Inspection/Search
Fine up to ₹1 lakh + Imprisonment up to 6 months
Refusing entry to authorised inspectors, hiding records, tampering with evidence during search. Adds to underlying offence.
§35 read with §37
Offences by Companies / Associations
Each person in charge (directors, trustees, key functionaries) is personally liable
The "directing mind" of the organisation cannot hide behind corporate veil. This is the rule that makes trustees personally criminally liable.
§38
False Statements in Applications / Returns
Fine + Imprisonment up to 6 months (in addition to cancellation)
Any false information in FC-4 annual return, registration application, prior permission application — even if the FC itself was used correctly.
§33
Failure to Maintain Records / Accounts
Fine up to ₹1 lakh
Not maintaining separate FC accounts, not keeping vouchers, not having audited accounts. Often an early trigger for deeper investigation.
⚠️ Personal Criminal Liability of Trustees / Directors

Section 37 of FCRA is its sharpest weapon against individuals. When an organisation (company, trust, society) commits an offence, every person who at the time was in charge of, and responsible for, the conduct of the organisation is personally guilty. The only defence is proving the offence was committed without that person's knowledge and with all due diligence. In practice, this means all trustees, directors, and office bearers face personal arrest and trial for FCRA violations of their organisation.

ℹ️ What Compounding Means

Compounding is essentially a settlement mechanism. The violating organisation pays a penalty amount to MHA, and in return, no criminal prosecution is launched for that specific violation. Compounding does NOT erase the violation from records — it still appears as a compliance default and can affect renewal. Major substantive violations (receiving FC illegally, using FC for wrong purpose) are typically NOT compoundable.

📦 Inventory / materials

The Prosecution Process Under FCRA

1️⃣
Intelligence Input / Complaint / Inspection
Enforcement begins with an IB input, a citizen complaint, a media report, or a routine inspection finding a red flag. MHA initiates internal inquiry.
2️⃣
Show Cause Notice (SCN)
MHA issues an SCN to the organisation and key functionaries stating the alleged violation and asking them to explain. The organisation has a specified time (usually 30-60 days) to respond in writing.
3️⃣
Suspension (if warranted)
MHA may simultaneously suspend the registration to freeze the situation pending inquiry. FC accounts may be frozen by directions to the bank.
4️⃣
Personal Hearing & Inquiry
Organisation can request (and should always request) a personal hearing. Present documents, explanations, witnesses. The inquiry officer must give genuine opportunity to be heard.
5️⃣
Cancellation Order / Compounding / Revocation
After inquiry: (a) Revocation of suspension + warning, (b) Compounding for minor violations, or (c) Cancellation + referral for criminal prosecution.
6️⃣
Appellate Remedy — High Court Writ
There is no statutory appeal against cancellation within FCRA itself. The remedy is a writ petition before the jurisdictional High Court challenging the order on grounds of perversity, procedural irregularity, or constitutional violation.
